Source: Downrange Analysis for Optimal Lunar Soft Precision Landing. MSc thesis, Politecnico di Torino, ~2022-2023. https://webthesis.biblio.polito.it/26955/1/tesi.pdf
(Close paraphrase of the public source, for testing. The thesis's own novelty is trajectory optimization; its GNC/HDA content is a state-of-the-art review, so redundancy and fault handling are described generically.)

The lunar landing mission requires a highly accurate GNC system. The system must be able to detect and avoid hazards, such as rocks and craters, through a Hazard Detection and Avoidance (HDA) system, reliably find an alternate landing site, and command a diversion. A vision-based relative navigation system is also needed. Two visual-navigation types are distinguished: absolute navigation, matching onboard camera features to a stored database or orbital topographic data, limited by illumination; and relative navigation, used in the final landing phase to locate the vehicle relative to features in onboard images. The HDA characterizes the sensed surface for hazardous features; to overcome the limitations of camera-based hazard detection, an imaging LIDAR is used, though imaging LIDARs often build a terrain image through a scanning mechanism requiring compensation for the lander's motion. The HDA may command diversions to alternative sites if the current one is unsafe; a constrained trajectory keeps the landing site within the sensors' field of view given viewing-angle and throttle limits during retargeting. The guidance computes a reachability and fuel map of feasible diversions, used to confirm or re-select the site. The Apollo baseline reviewed uses an IMU, periscope optics for IMU star alignment, and a four-beam landing radar (one ranging, three velocity) for altitude, altitude-rate, and horizontal velocity, plus an abort guidance system as a redundant GNC.
