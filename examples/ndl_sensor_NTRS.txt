The Navigation Doppler Lidar (NDL) is a coherent Doppler lidar providing ground-relative vector velocity and altitude/range for autonomous precision landing. Operating from over four kilometers altitude, the NDL obtains velocity and range precision reaching 2 cm/s and 2 m respectively. It transmits three laser beams at different pointing angles toward the ground, measuring range and velocity along each beam using a frequency-modulated continuous-wave (FMCW) technique; the three line-of-sight measurements are combined to determine the three components of the vehicle velocity vector and the altitude relative to ground. The modulation waveform has three segments: an up-ramp chirp, a constant-frequency segment, and a down-ramp chirp; the constant-frequency segment produces a Doppler velocity used to eliminate data dropouts and remove measurement ambiguities. Performance was demonstrated through ground tests, helicopter flights, and onboard autonomous rocket-powered test vehicles operating open- and closed-loop with a GNC system. Past planetary landings relied on radar; the NDL is offered as an alternative with higher precision and lower size, weight, and power. The laser-based NDL does not suffer measurement perturbation from terrain features or signal ambiguity from side lobes, and is far less susceptible to signal clutter such as returns from the lander structure or jettisoned components, enabling a well-controlled, lower-load touchdown.
