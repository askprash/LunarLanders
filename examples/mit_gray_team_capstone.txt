(Close paraphrase of the public source, for testing.)

The trajectory is divided into lunar-orbit, transfer-orbit, and powered-descent phases. Powered descent begins at periapsis at 15.24 km and consists of two gravity turns plus a final hover phase initiated at 100 m altitude with 1.23 m/s velocity, with about 110 s of hover propellant to land in a desirable location. Our goal is to utilize hardware that enables completely autonomous navigation. The main navigation unit is an IMU (three orthogonal accelerometers and gyros); because integration errors build over time, the IMU is updated by star trackers (sun sensors were rejected as less accurate), plus ground tracking via DSN. A ground-truth altitude measurement is provided by a landing radar; radar was chosen over LIDAR because it is proven and has no dust problems, while existing LIDAR maps are utilized in the computer algorithms. The on-board GNC baseline sensor suite consists of an IMU, landing radar, and star trackers (Honeywell MIMU, HG9550 landing radar, SED26 star tracker). Control and estimation use a minimum-time/minimum-fuel LQR controller with an Extended Kalman Filter; the state includes position, velocity, attitude, rate, mass, and inertia. Cameras give landing-site visibility for crew/operator redesignation from 5.3 km. Critical-descent altitude of about 10 m is the minimum where abort-to-orbit with the ascent stage is still possible; below it the engines cannot spin up in time. Monte-Carlo CEP is about 290 m; the acceptable error band is plus or minus 50 m. Known reliability issues with the RL-10 engine are acknowledged and assumed remedied by mission time.
