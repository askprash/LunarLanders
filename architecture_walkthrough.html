Use landing history to ask review questions.

This prototype links source-cited lunar landing incidents to review-readiness questions for future lander concepts. It is designed to support human review, not to certify a vehicle or replace mission-assurance judgment.

The short version

A reviewer should understand the whole architecture before reading the detailed method documents. The pipeline has three jobs.

01

Preserve evidence

Start from reports, disclosures, papers, and technical sources. Keep source links, caveats, confidence, and validation status attached to every reusable lesson.

02

Retrieve precedents

Run an auditable deterministic pass before any language-model reasoning. The first pass should name the candidate precedent and why it was retrieved.

03

Ask for review evidence

Turn supported precedent matches into questions for SRR, PDR, CDR, integration, test, and flight-readiness discussions.

Design text

“The lander rejects altitude measurements that diverge sharply from the propagated estimate during terminal descent.”

Retrieved precedent

Hakuto‑R M1: public disclosure describes altitude measurement rejection after terrain-driven divergence.

Review question

At CDR, what test evidence shows the altitude filter handles terrain and landing-site changes without locking onto a stale estimate?

Workflow at a glance

The workflow preserves the evidence chain, retrieves relevant precedents, then asks reviewers for evidence that the future design has handled the same class of risk.

Pipeline from source to review question

Each stage has a maturity label. The labels separate source evidence, curated interpretation, deterministic matching, language-model interpretation, and human review.

LLM boundaries

The language model is useful only after retrieval. It helps interpret realistic ConOps prose, but the source record remains the evidence base.

Allowed

  • Interpret realistic ConOps prose and vocabulary variation.
  • Compare the ConOps to retrieved precedents.
  • Explain caveats and proximate-cause differences.
  • Reject weak deterministic matches.
  • Turn supported concerns into readiness gaps and evidence requests.

Not allowed

  • Invent precedents, sources, quotes, or mission facts.
  • Upgrade confidence without evidence.
  • Claim certification, compliance, or flight readiness.
  • Erase important differences in proximate cause.
  • Treat missing text about a mitigation as proof that the design has the risk.
Limitations and failure modes

The source corpus may be weak, secondary, incomplete, or not authoritative. Extraction can misquote or omit uncertainty. A failure class can be generalized too far across missions with different proximate causes. Deterministic matching can miss paraphrases or treat a keyword as a mitigation. The language model can overstate confidence or reason beyond retrieved evidence. A reader can mistake review-readiness coaching for certification.

Those risks are why the architecture preserves sources, caveats, maturity labels, deterministic retrieval, and human/domain-expert review.
