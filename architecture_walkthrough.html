LLM boundary

The LLM is useful, but it is not the source of truth. It receives retrieved evidence and a deterministic report, then helps with semantic interpretation and explanation.

Allowed

  • Interpret realistic ConOps prose and vocabulary variation.
  • Compare the ConOps to retrieved precedents.
  • Explain caveats and proximate-cause differences.
  • Reject weak deterministic matches.
  • Turn supported concerns into readiness gaps and evidence requests.

Not allowed

  • Invent precedents, sources, quotes, or mission facts.
  • Silently upgrade confidence.
  • Claim certification, compliance, or flight readiness.
  • Erase important differences in proximate cause.
  • Treat absence of mitigation in the supplied text as proof of risk.
Limitations and pipeline failure modes

This walkthrough is intentionally optimistic about how AI can help, but the method can fail. The source corpus may be weak, secondary, incomplete, or not authoritative. Extraction quotes or page references may be wrong. A failure class may be overgeneralized across missions with different proximate causes. Deterministic matching can miss paraphrases or create brittle keyword matches. The LLM can overstate confidence or interpret beyond retrieved evidence. A reader may mistake review-readiness coaching for certification.

Those risks are why the architecture preserves sources, caveats, artifact maturity labels, deterministic retrieval, and human/domain-expert review.
